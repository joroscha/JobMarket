# Songscription Meeting Cheat Sheet

## Purpose

This is not a job interview. The goal is to:

- get to know Andrew
- understand the company and product direction
- explore where my background overlaps naturally

## Quick Context

- Songscription uses AI to turn audio into editable sheet music and related outputs.
- The company appears to be very early stage and founder-led.
- The product likely sits at the intersection of transcription, music learning, arrangement, and musician workflow tools.

## Product Signals From FAQ

- The product is broader than transcription alone: it supports audio, MIDI, YouTube links, and direct recording as inputs.
- Outputs include sheet music, MIDI, guitar tabs, MusicXML, Guitar Pro, and a piano roll.
- The product is explicitly designed for musicians, teachers, learners, composers, and people who may not read notation.
- Beginner usability seems central: the piano roll is positioned as a major accessibility feature.
- Current strength is single-instrument transcription, even from multi-instrument recordings.
- Full-band and orchestral transcription are not yet supported, but are on the roadmap.
- The notation system is optimized for Western notation, though they see demand from non-Western users and styles.
- The roadmap explicitly mentions chords, arrangement, and leveling.
- The business model is freemium, with education/nonprofit discounts and an enterprise contact path.

- I am not a core systems person.
- My edge is understanding adoption, product value, evaluation, and behavior change.
- I can speak credibly about musicians, creators, experimentation, and what makes AI tools actually useful in practice.

## Good Questions

### Product and User

- Who do you think is the core user today?
- Is the product mainly for learners, teachers, performers, arrangers, or a mix?
- What user problem do you think Songscription solves best right now?
- What have you learned from users that surprised you?
- How important is beginner usability relative to notation accuracy?
- Is the piano roll a major driver of activation or retention?
- Which users get the most value today: learners, teachers, transcribers, or creators?

### Company Direction

- What is the longer-term vision for the company?
- Do you see Songscription primarily as a transcription tool, or as a broader music workflow or learning platform?
- What does success look like over the next 12 to 24 months?
- Which roadmap item matters most right now: chords, arrangement, leveling, or multi-instrument support?

### Evaluation and Product Value

- How do you think about product quality beyond transcription accuracy?
- How do you know when the output is actually useful for musicians?
- Are there questions about adoption, repeated use, or user value that you wish you understood better?
- What are the biggest current failure modes: pitch, rhythm, polyphony, separation, or notation usability?
- How do you evaluate "good enough" for product use rather than just model performance?

### Adjacent Product Space

- Do you think arrangement, accompaniment, or suggestion tools are a natural extension?
- How do you think about adjacent generative music features, given that they seem product-adjacent but also more controversial?

## Good Talking Points To Bring Up

### My AI Music Experiment

- In one of my experiments on AI-assisted music ideation, I have wondered whether giving participants sheet-music representations of the AI output would have changed how useful or creative the ideas felt.
- That seems closely related to how representation shapes product value for musicians.

### My Fit

- My background is strongest where product questions meet rigorous measurement.
- I think a lot about how to evaluate whether an AI tool changes behavior, improves outcomes, or creates real value for users.

## If He Asks How I Could Be Useful

- user and product evaluation
- experimentation
- adoption and retention analysis
- measuring value creation for musicians and learners
- translating ambiguous product questions into empirical studies

## Things To Avoid

- do not pretend to be a systems expert
- do not give a long monologue about my research
- do not force the conversation into a job ask too early
- do not jump straight into controversial generative music questions without first understanding their product priorities
